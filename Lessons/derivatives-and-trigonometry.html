Derivatives and Trigonometry

Consider the function \(f(x)=\sin(x)\) The function \(\sin(x)\) is different from polynomial functions because they are periodic. If the function repeats itself, then the derivative repeats itself as well. Let's look at a table of slopes to find the derivative:
\(x\)\(f(x)\)Slope
\(0\)\(0\)\(1\)
\(\frac{\pi}{2}\)\(1\)\(0\)
\(\pi\)\(0\)\(-1\)
\(\frac{3\pi}{2}\)\(-1\)\(0\)
\(2\pi\)\(0\)\(1\)
You may notice that the slope of \(\sin(x)\) is very similar to \(\sin(x)\) itself! If you take any value of the function above and shift it up one row, you get the slope of the function.

This is because the derivative of \(\sin(x)\) is \(\cos(x)\). For a more detailed proof, look here.

Look at the graphs of \(\sin(x)\) and \(\cos(x)\) and observe this. $$f(x)=\sin(x)$$ $$f^{\prime}(x)=\cos(x)$$ You may notice that the derivative of \(\sin(x)\) is just the function "shifted left" by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\). And this is correct. This is true of both sine and cosine. In fact, because of this, the derivative of \(\cos(x)\) is \(-\sin(x)\), the derivative of \(-\sin(x)\) is \(-\cos(x)\), and the derivative of \(-\cos(x)\) is \(\sin(x)\). Trig really feels like a small world, sometimes.

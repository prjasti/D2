Left and Right Limits

You now know that limits are times when functions look like they should have a value at a certain spot but really don't. As seen in the first lesson, a limit can fill these holes in a graph by approaching a value.

But sometimes limits depend on the way we look at a function. Look at the following function: $$f(x)=\frac{|x|}{x}$$ Here, there is no y value at \(x=0\). But something interesting is going on with the limits.


Let's try to figure out what the value at zero is by looking at numbers less than zero:
\(x\)\(f(x)\)
\(-0.1\)\(-1\)
\(-0.01\)\(-1\)
\(-0.001\)\(-1\)
\(0\)\(\textrm{undefined}\)
It appears as if from the left, \(f(0)\) should be -1. This is shown in calculus as: $$\lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{|x|}{x}=-1$$ Where the minus sign (-) means that we're looking at numbers lower than zero.


Now let's look at numbers greater than zero:
\(x\)\(f(x)\)
\(0\)\(\textrm{undefined}\)
\(0.001\)\(1\)
\(0.01\)\(1\)
\(0.1\)\(1\)
On the right, it seems like \(f(0)\) should be +1. Similar to the previous example, this is shown as: $$\lim_{x\to 0^+}\frac{|x|}{x}=1$$ Where the plus sign (+) means that we're looking at numbers greater than zero.

What we just did is called a one sided limit. A one sided limit is the limit of a function only looking at one side of the graph: a left limits focuses on values to the left of the limit, and a right limit is based off of those to the right.

However, because the limit on the left and right sides don't equal, the two sided limit (for both the left and right sides) does not exist. $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{|x|}{x}=\textrm{does not exist}$$
Let's look at another example. $$f(x)=x^2\ |\ x< 0$$ $$f(x)=x + 1\ |\ x \geq 0$$ This is a tougher example.

From the left, the graph is \(f(x)=x^2\), and the function approaches zero as we approach \(x=0\) from the left side.
Therefore, $$\lim_{x\to 0^-}f(x)=0$$ From the right, the graph is \(f(x)=x+1\), and the function approaches 1 from the right side.
Therefore, $$\lim_{x\to 0^+}f(x)=1$$

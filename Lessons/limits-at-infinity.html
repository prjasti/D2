Limits at Infinity

There comes a point in our lives when someone finally tells us the truth about life: infinity is not actually a number. When we look at a graph, we can try as hard as we can to reach infinity but all we do is approach it. Do you see where I'm going with this?

And that's why we use limits to find values of functions at infinity.

Let's start with the following function: $$f(x)=\frac{1}{x}$$ We can usually look at a graph and predict what it's end behavior is. We see in this example that \(\frac{1}{x}\) keeps getting smaller, getting infinitesimaly close to 0 without reaching it. Try tracing the graph above and see for yourself if you don't believe me.
\(x\)\(f(x)\)
\(10\)\(.1\)
\(100\)\(.01\)
\(1000\)\(.001\)
\(10000\)\(.0001\)

Since the numbers approach zero, this limit can be written as $$\lim_{x\to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$$ Now for the algebra behind this.

To figure out the limit of a rational function - a fraction with polynomials in the numberator and denominator - we must comapre the highest degree term in both the numerator and denominator as shown:
Numerator Degree vs Denominator DegreeEffectExample
Numerator is SmallerZero\(\frac{3x}{x^2+1}\to \infty = 0\)
EqualFraction of Coefficients\(\frac{3x}{x+1} \to \infty = \frac{3}{1}=3\)
Denominator is SmallerInfinity\(\frac{3x^2}{x+1} \to \infty = \infty\)
Here are the three functions shown in the table so you can see what happens: $$f(x)=\frac{3x}{x^2+1}$$

$$f(x)=\frac{3x}{x+1}$$

$$f(x)=\frac{3x^2}{x+1}$$ Why does this work? You may ask...

Not all infinities are created equal. Even though \(x\) and \(x^2\) are both infinity at infinity, \(x^2\) is way bigger. This is because \(x^2\) gets bigger faster than \(x\) at large numbers. We know this because \(x^2\) has a higher degree, or exponent.

To figure out these limits, we must look at the term of highest degree in the numerator and denominator. For example, look at the third function above: $$f(x)=\frac{3x^2}{x+1}$$ In the denominator, we could care less about the \(+1\) in \(x+1\) since it is smaller than \(x\) at infinity. This simplifies the equation to $$f(x)=\frac{3x^2}{x}=3x$$ As \(x\) approaches infinity, \(3x\) also approaches infinity.

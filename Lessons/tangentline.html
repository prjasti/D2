The Tangent Line Problem

A tangent line is a line that just barely touches — but does not cross over — another line. For example, look at the function $$f(x)=x^{2}$$

You may have noticed that the x-axis, in fact, is tangent to the parabola above.

But how do we find a tangent line to a function? And how do we know the slope of the line at the tangent?

To find a tangent line, we make a similar type of line called a secant line and then turn it into a tangent line. For example, the graph below is also of \(f(x)=x^{2}\)

The secant line is a line on a curve that intersects the curve at two points. Let's call them \((x_{1}, y_{1})\) and \((x_{2}, y_{2})\). You may recall that the slope between two points is rise over run, or $$m=\frac{rise}{run}=\frac{Δy}{Δx}=\frac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}$$

In this graph, since the secant line goes through \((0,0)\) and \((1,1)\), the slope is $$m=\frac{1-0}{1-0}=1$$

Try using the interactive graph below to make a secant line through different points on the graph.

To make this secant line, which goes through two points, become a tangent line, which goes through one point, we must bring both points as close to each other as possible. For example, the points \((0.9, 0.81)\) and \((1, 1)\) are really close and the secant line between them have a slope of $$\frac{1-0.81}{1-0.9}=\frac{0.19}{0.1}=1.9$$

But what happens if we try to make both points at exactly the same spot? What if they're both \((1,1)\)? We can't calculate the slope of this line since the slope equation will give an answer of \(\frac{0}{0}\) — or indeterminate. $$\frac{1-1}{1-1}=\frac{0}{0}=\textrm{indeterminate}$$

Instead, mathematicians make the two points approach each other: they become infinitesimally close so that the answer is the same but the denominator is not exactly zero, making the answer defined. In calculus, this is called a limit, represented as $$m=\frac{1-y_{1}}{1-x_{1}}=\lim_{x \to 1}\frac{1-f(x)}{1-x}$$

Do you think you found the answer to the limit above? At the point \((1,1)\), the slope of \(f(x) = x^{2}\) is exactly 2. This can be solved by creating the secant line and making the points approach each other, or by solving the limit analytically.

Just for completeness, let's solve for the equation of the tangent line. You may remember that the point-slope form of a linear equation is \(y=m(x-x_{1})+y_{1}\). All we need to do is plug in \(2\) for \(m\) and \((1, 1)\) for \((x_{1}, y_{1})\), as shown below: $$y=2(x-1)+1$$

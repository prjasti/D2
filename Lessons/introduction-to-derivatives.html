Introduction To Derivatives

If you've ever looked at a linear function in the form \(y=mx+b\), you've probably been told that \(m\) refers to the slope of the function. And it does. For example, in the equation \(f(x)=3x+1\), the slope can be written as \(f^\prime (x) = 3\) But how do we get the slope of a nonlinear function?

A derivative of a function states the slope of that function at different x-values. As seen in the previous lesson, the slope of an equation can be found from the following equation: $$\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$ This is because it is based off of the equation for slope, returning the slope of the tangent line of the function at each value of x.

Take a look at the graph of \(f(x)=x^3\) The derivative, or slope of the graph, can be represented as $$f^\prime (x)=\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(x+h)^3-(x)^3}{h}$$ This simplifies to $$\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3}{h}=\lim_{h \to 0}(3x^2+3xh+h^2)=3x^2$$ Look at the two graphs below (the function and its derivative) and observe how the slope of the first curve (\(x^3\)) is always the value of the second curve (\(3x^2\)). $$f(x)=x^3$$ $$f^\prime (x)=3x^2$$ This means that \(3x^2\) is the derviative of \(x^3\).

Now for some basic properties of derivatives: Look at the functions \(f(x)=x^3\) and \(f(x)=2x^3\) Try to observe how the slope of \(2x^3\) to the slope of \(x^3\). Did you see that it doubled? This is because doubling a function doubles its derivative as well. In fact, multiplying a function by any number multiplies the derivative by that number, too.

What about addition, though?

Look at the function \(f(x)=x^3+3x\) How does the slope of this function compare to that of \(x^3\)? Because the derivative of \(3x\) is 3, we can add 3 to the slope of \(x^3\) to get the slope of \(x^3+3x\). This is another property of limits. If a function is a sum of 2 different things, the derivative of the function is the sum of the derivatives of the things in the function. Here, the derivative of \(x^3+3x\) is the sum of the derivatives of \(x^3\) and \(3x\), making the derivative equal to $$f^{\prime}(x)=3x^2+3$$

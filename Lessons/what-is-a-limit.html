What Is A Limit?

When learning algebra, you probably learned what a domain is, and how some functions don't have y-values for all values of x. Take a look at the graph $$f(x)=\frac{x^3+1}{x+1}$$ Your algebra instincts may tell you that the domain of this function is $$x\neq -1$$ because at -1, the denominator is 0. And you would be right. Maybe it's just a feeling, but you know that can't be true.

Doesn't it look like the point \((-1,3)\) is on the graph? I mean, it's right there!

And that's where limits come in. With limits, the domain can be extended to numbers like -1 by predicting the value of the function at that spot. In calculus, we push the limits of functions. \(f(x)\) has no value at -1, but we can test -0.9, -0.99, and other similar numbers that approach -1 to find the value of the function.
To show this strategy, here's a table of values of \(f(x)\) that approach -1:
\(x\)\(f(x)\)
\(-0.9\)\(2.71\)
\(-0.99\)\(2.9701\)
\(-1\)\(\textrm{undefined}\)
\(-1.1\)\(3.0301\)
\(-0.99\)\(3.31\)

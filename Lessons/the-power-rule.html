The Power Rule

Although we can use the formula mentioned in the previous lesson to find the derivative of a function, there is a shortcut to finding the derivatives of polynomial functions. Look at the graph of \(f(x)=x^3\)
As shown earlier, the derivative is \(f^\prime (x)=2x^3\). What you might notice is that the exponent decreases by one, and the derivative is multiplied by the original exponent. In \(x^3\), the \(x^3\) is replaced with an \(x^2\) and then \(x^2\) is multiplied by \(3\) to become \(3x^2\). This is a real pattern with derivatives, and it is called the power rule because it works for \(x\) raised to any power. While the proof is somewhat complicating if you are unfamiliar with derivatives, the power rule makes sense when you look at a graph. Look at the following graphs of \(x^2\) and its derivative, \(2x\). This even works for negative numbers! Take the function $$f(x)=\frac{1}{x}$$ This is because \(\frac{1}{x}\) can be rewritten as \(x^{-1}\). The \(-1\) becomes a \(-2\) to make \(x^{-2}\), and the equation is multiplied by \(-1\) because that was the original exponent. This gives the final answer: $$f^{\prime}(x)=-x^{-2}=\frac{-1}{x^2}$$ See for yourself! The graph below shows the slope of \(\frac{1}{x}\) at all points. And that's it!
